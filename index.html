I am currently a Research Associate in the Department of Computer Science & Engineering at the University of Minnesota, working on autonomous systems, teleoperation, and network-aware intelligent robotics. Previously, I served as a Postdoctoral Research Fellow at DGIST (South Korea), where I worked on Level-4 autonomous driving, including localization, 3D mapping, sensor calibration, and perception systems.

My research lies at the intersection of robotics, mobility, and artificial intelligence, with a strong focus on real-world deployment challenges. I develop algorithms and system-level solutions that enable intelligent agents to perceive complex environments, collaborate with humans, and operate reliably under communication and sensing constraints. My research interests include multimodal perception, remote driving, AV-edge collaborative intelligence, vision-language reasoning, and communication-aware autonomy. I have contributed to multiple research projects spanning autonomous driving, V2X-enabled safety, and human-in-the-loop systems, with applications in transportation, robotics, and intelligent infrastructure.

ReACT2: Robots That Know When to Ask: VLM-Driven Reasoning for Human-on-Demand Multi-Robot Teleoperation
Ajay Kumar and Rushikesh Zende, Vivek Kumar and Lalitaditya Divakarla, Kalyan Sai
IROS 2026 (Under review)
Recent deployments of autonomous robots across domains such as logistics, delivery, and field operations highlight the importance of teleoperation for timely human intervention in safety-critical environments. However, existing systems rely on heuristic or uncertainty-based triggers, lacking semantic understanding and efficient prioritization when one operator supervises multiple robots. We propose a vision-language reasoning–driven teleoperation framework that enables robots to request human assistance based on context-aware semantic understanding. The approach leverages a vision-language model (VLM) aligned using Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) to calibrate intervention decisions. Evaluations across diverse scenarios—including social interactions, dynamic obstacles, and multi-robot coordination—demonstrate improved intervention efficiency, reduced unnecessary operator engagement, and enhanced safety compared to conventional methods. This work offers a scalable approach toward reliable human-in-the-loop autonomy.
Remote Driving as a Transitional Path to Autonomous Mobility Deployments
Ajay Kumar and and Rushikesh Zende, Vivek Kumar and Lalitaditya Divakarla, Kalyan Sai, Dipan Kumar
Add journal / conference, year
This project investigates remote driving and human-in-the-loop connected and autonomous vehicle (CAV) technologies as a practical and scalable pathway to address persistent mobility, safety, and accessibility challenges in rural transportation systems. While autonomous vehicle deployments have advanced rapidly in urban environments, rural roads present unique technical, infrastructural, and social challenges that limit the feasibility of full autonomy, particularly for elderly and mobility-impaired populations. The proposed research develops a hybrid autonomy framework that combines onboard automation with low-latency remote supervision using adaptive video streaming over heterogeneous networks. The project integrates system design, network performance evaluation, and human-centered studies to show how remote driving can safely enhance accessibility and accelerate rural CAV deployment. Through field experiments, community engagement, and open-source dissemination, this work aims to enable inclusive, economically viable autonomous mobility services and create new deployment opportunities for public agencies, startups, and private service providers in underserved rural regions.

Mobile EV Charging Robots: A Scalable Solution for Urban Environment
Ajay Kumar and collaborators
Add journal / conference, year
Fixed EV charging infrastructure faces major challenges in harsh environments—snow, ice, corrosion, heat, and space constraints limit accessibility and reliability, especially in dense urban areas. To address this, we are developing a Mobile EV Charging Robot that delivers on-demand charging by autonomously navigating parking environments using a high-capacity, swappable or rechargeable battery system. The robot can be dispatched via a mobile app and operates in both indoor garages and outdoor lots, eliminating the need for fixed infrastructure. With EV adoption rapidly growing and public charging lagging—particularly in cold-climate regions like Minnesota—this solution targets urban users without home charging, commercial parking facilities, and fleet operators. By offering flexible, weather-resilient charging, the system provides a scalable alternative to traditional infrastructure and improves accessibility in space- and climate-constrained environments.

SICK Mine Guard
Ajay Kumar and collaborators
Add journal / conference, year
SICK Mine Guard is an award-winning project (Winner of the SICK $10K Challenge) that focuses on enhancing safety and situational awareness in mining environments using advanced sensing and intelligent perception. The system leverages LiDAR-based sensing and real-time data processing to detect hazards, monitor worker presence, and enable proactive risk mitigation in low-visibility and dynamic underground conditions. By integrating robust perception with intelligent decision support, Mine Guard aims to prevent accidents, improve operational safety, and support autonomous or assisted navigation in challenging industrial settings. The project demonstrates how AI-driven sensing solutions can be effectively deployed in safety-critical environments to protect human workers and optimize industrial operations.

SPOT Miner Rescue Robotic Dog
Ajay Kumar and collaborators
Add journal / conference, year
Spot Mine Rescue Dog is a robotic safety and exploration project developed at Missouri S&T, leveraging the quadruped robot Spot for hazardous underground and mining environments. The system is designed to assist in search-and-rescue operations by enabling remote navigation, real-time situational awareness, and autonomous exploration in areas that are unsafe or inaccessible to humans. Equipped with advanced sensors such as cameras and LiDAR, the platform supports environment mapping, obstacle detection, and victim localization under low-visibility and structurally complex conditions. By combining teleoperation with intelligent perception, the project demonstrates how legged robotic systems can enhance safety, accelerate emergency response, and reduce risk to human rescuers in critical mining scenarios.
